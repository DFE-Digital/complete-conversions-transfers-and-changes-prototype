Choosing check boxes or radio buttons

After deciding to present all actions for a task on a single page, we had to consider if radio buttons were still appropriate. Checkboxes might provide a better solution.

Thinking about alternatives to questions and radio buttons

While asking questions and providing radio buttons for response options works well for many services, it felt like it might not be the best experience for users in this instance.

The work caseworkers will carry out on our system is repetitive, and can require them to go back and forth, in and out of tasks, to update them as tasks progress.

Re-entering tasks and flicking through multiple questions was going to be too time-consuming. Now we felt that framing actions and questions and providing yes or no answers wouldn't give users the clear and quick view of task progress that research had found they wanted.

As a team, we felt that it was sensible to try an alternative and see how that tested with users.

Collaborating to define a design template for a task

We ran a session where members of the team, from across all disciplines, sketched out how we envisaged the actions within a task (opens in a new tab) .

Independently created, these ideas converged around a common approach.

Actions used clear, active language to instruct the user on what to do to complete the task.

Actions had some guidance that provided context and often linked to useful resources, such as forms, SharePoint locations or detailed guidance documents.

Every action also had at least one checkbox that users could select when a piece of work had been done.

Our hypothesis was that, based on the consistent user feedback around the idea of ticking things off to keep track of progress, that this would meet their mental model.

Checkboxes were what users consistently asked for

Research had consistently brought up the idea that users want to tick off tasks, or actions within a task.

That knowledge from previous research observations and playbacks informed each of our sketches.

A checkbox-based thing felt like the natural design to answer the needs to manage workload and understand project progress.

Our informed assumption was that using checkboxes would also help users identify what steps to take to complete a task and get a view of task progress. This could be particularly helpful for new caseworkers, people returning to the role after a prolonged absence or those who struggled to remember how to complete tasks for any reason.

We felt that another advantage of checkboxes was that they could give us an opportunity to be more direct, active and instructive in our language. This could help to make content succinct, simpler to read and actions could be used as guidance or instructions rather than simply querying if a thing had been done.

It would also mean we could bring the existing prototype closer to the proof on concept that had been created in the build.

What we'll do next

Next, we'll iterate the prototype to switch from radio buttons to check boxes.

We'll also start to check the order of the tasks on the task list.

Then, we'll carry out research with users to test our assumptions, iterate the design, and then use those findings and designs to expand on the build.

That will lead us to an important milestone where the prototype and build move beyond an exploratory phase, to a point where they are closely aligned and the design and development work is tightly focussed and linked together.
